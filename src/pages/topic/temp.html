Communication

Ask Questions!  No question is too stupid to ask. Trust your instincts.

"Speak Up: Prevent Errors in Your Care" from the Joint Commission5

1. Write down all questions to ask your healthcare providers or ask someone to do it for you.6

"Speak Up: Ask Your Advocate to Speak Up" from the Joint Commission7

"Bill Lee: Had to Ask Questions...Dying Not Acceptable." by AHRQ Health TV8

2. Don't distract the nurse while they are giving medications. Ask questions before!6

3. Bring the patient's medication and allergy list to show the doctor and nurse.9

4. Ask questions respectfully about everything. For example9:

  • Why do I need this medication? 
  • What is it for? 
  • What will it do?
  • Is this the right dose?
  • Is this medication for me?
  • Can you explain that to me again?

"Molly Glassman: You Have To Ask Questions." by AHRQ Health TV10

Medical Dictionary from the National Library of Medicine

5. Ask all medical staff for their business card.6

Who's Who in the Hospital by the Empowered Patient Coalition

6. Write down or have someone write down the medical staff’s names if no card is given.6

7. Keep a journal or write in your phone, mobile device or computer with the answers given6:

  • What is the test, procedure, medication and/or surgery for?
  • How many times have you done this procedure, test or procedure?
  • When will the results be back?
  • Why do I need this treatment?
  • Are there any alternatives?
  • What are the complications?
  • How do you spell the name of the drug? 
  • What are the side effects?
  • Will this new drug interact with any of the medications I’m already taking?

10 Questions You Should Know from the Agency for Healthcare and Research Quality

8. Be aware that the medical team may not speak with each other, as they should.6

"Questions are the Answer" from the Safe Care Campaign11

9. If a doctor tells you your medication or treatment plan is or has changed, find out from your nurse if the orders have been written.6

10. Doctors must write the hospital orders for drugs, tests, procedures and the treatment plan.6

Tests and Procedures from the Mayo Clinic

11. Ask your doctor: “Could this be anything else?”12

Checklist for Getting the Right Diagnosis from the National Patient Safety Foundation

"Talking Openly With Your Medical Provider" by the National Institutes of Health13

6 Tips for Talking With Your Doctor from The Empowered Patient

12. At the change of shift ask the patient's nurses to compare notes at the patient's bedside.9

13. Call the nurse if the patient doesn't seem right or if the alarm goes off.6,9

14. Call the nurse if the patient has6,9:

  • Extreme pain
  • Fever or chills
  • Redness, swelling, &/or pus
  • Confusion
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Very slow breathing
  • Dizziness
  • Unusual sweating
  • Rash or hives
  • Drop in blood pressure
  • High or low temperature
  • Changes in heart rate
  • Changes in breathing
  • Decrease in urine
  • Patient doesn’t look right

Listen to your gut or intuition. If no one is listening to you, speak with the Nurse Manager.6,9

Adult and children normal vital signs are below14 15:

  • Normal body temperature is 97.8 degrees Fahrenheit to 99 degrees Fahrenheit. 
  • Normal adult heart rate is 60 to 100 beats per minute.
  • Normal adult breathing rate is 12 to 16 breathes per minute.
  • Normal adult blood pressure is less than 120/80 mm Hg.
